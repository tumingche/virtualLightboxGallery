By mixing pigments with a range of media within the same painting, Rothko created subtle variations of matte and gloss that further enriched the play of light on the surface. For example, the orange shape in Panel One is often described as more “luminous” than the crimson background. This glowing effect is achieved with the use of whole egg as a binding medium in the orange paint, which contrasts with the matte, animal-glue-based crimson of the background.

In 1962, celebrated abstract expressionist artist Mark Rothko completed a monumental series of paintings commissioned for the penthouse dining room of Harvard University’s newly designed Holyoke Center (now called the Richard A. and Susan F. Smith Campus Center). In a few short years, high levels of natural light coming through the penthouse’s floor-to-ceiling windows caused Rothko’s paintings to fade. By the 1970s, the five paintings showed disfiguring patterns of color loss that disrupted the visual unity central to Rothko’s conception. Considered too altered to be exhibited, the delicate paintings were removed from their intended architectural setting and placed in storage.

The exhibition Mark Rothko’s Harvard Murals (on display at the Harvard Art Museums throughJuly 26, 2015) proposes an innovative restoration method: the digital projection of precisely calibrated colored light as a tool to restore the appearance of the murals’ original colors. A team from the Harvard Art Museums’ Straus Center for Conservation and Technical Studies, led by conservation scientist Jens Stenger and in collaboration with scientists from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology and the University of Basel, Switzerland, devised this noninvasive approach that restores the color to Rothko’s murals, pixel by pixel, through a sophisticated scientific system of projected color compensation. This treatment returns an important Rothko mural series—and one of Harvard’s most important commissions of contemporary art—to public view and scholarship, allowing it to be experienced as a whole for the first time in decades.

Rothko blended his own paints to achieve the exact color and effect he desired. To create the Harvard Murals, he exploited both traditional pigments, such as Ultramarine Blue, and newer, synthetic pigments like Lithol Red, developed at the turn of the 20th century and used predominantly in the printing industry from the 1950s onward. The plum-colored paint he used for the background was a mixture of Ultramarine and Lithol Red in an animal-glue binding medium. In the dark motifs, Rothko used other pigments such as carbon-based black, Phthalocyanine blue (PB 15), and alizarin (PR83).

Rothko made revisions to his paintings, and several works bear visible traces of such rethinking. In Panel Five, for instance, he reduced the size of the nodules and top corners of the lighter shape by painting over the white with a mixture of Lithol Red and Ultramarine Blue. Early photos of the painting show that Rothko did not go to great pains to conceal these corrections. The changes are even more noticeable in the panel’s faded state: the Ultramarine Blue stands out visibly against the white, where the Lithol Red has faded completely from the purple mixture.
